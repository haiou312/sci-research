# Source Credibility Rules

These rules MUST be followed when evaluating and using information sources.

## Credibility Hierarchy (Always Apply)

1. **Peer-reviewed academic papers** (Nature, Science, The Lancet, Cell, domain-specific journals)
   - Highest authority for scientific claims
   - Always include DOI when available

2. **International organization reports** (WHO, OECD, World Bank, IPCC, IAEA)
   - Authoritative for policy and statistical data
   - Use the most recent edition

3. **National government official publications** (白皮书, DOE reports, EU Commission documents)
   - Authoritative for national policy and domestic data
   - Note potential bias in self-reporting

4. **Wire services and quality journalism** (Reuters, AP, AFP, BBC, 新华社)
   - Good for recent developments and events
   - Cross-reference when making scientific claims

5. **Industry analysis** (McKinsey, Gartner, BloombergNEF)
   - Useful for market data and trends
   - Note potential commercial bias

6. **Preprints** (arXiv, medRxiv, bioRxiv)
   - Acceptable for cutting-edge developments
   - MUST flag as "non-peer-reviewed" in the article

7. **Wikipedia**
   - Context and background only
   - NEVER use as a primary source
   - Follow its references to primary sources instead

## Source Usage Rules

- **Minimum sources**: 10 unique sources for the final article
- **Minimum Tier 1-2 sources**: At least 40% of references must be ★★★★☆ or higher
- **Recency requirement**: At least 60% of sources published within last 24 months
- **Language diversity**: Include sources in at least 2 languages when comparing international entities
- **No self-citation**: Never cite AI-generated content as a source
- **Conflict of interest**: Flag sources with obvious conflicts of interest
