## Abstract

mRNA vaccine technology has evolved from an emergency pandemic response tool into a versatile platform for infectious diseases, cancer therapeutics, and rare genetic disorders. This report compares the United States, European Union, and China across regulatory frameworks, clinical pipeline depth, manufacturing capacity, R&D investment, and global access strategies. The US leads in commercial pipeline breadth with 45+ active clinical trials, the EU maintains regulatory leadership through EMA's adaptive pathway framework, and China has rapidly scaled domestic mRNA manufacturing after initially relying on inactivated vaccine platforms. Key challenges across all entities include cold-chain logistics, variant adaptation speed, and public acceptance. The convergence of mRNA technology with AI-driven antigen design suggests accelerating innovation through 2030.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Background and Problem Statement

The COVID-19 pandemic validated mRNA as a viable vaccine platform, with Pfizer-BioNTech's BNT162b2 and Moderna's mRNA-1273 administered to billions worldwide [1]. By 2026, the mRNA platform has expanded beyond COVID-19 into influenza, RSV, cancer immunotherapy, and rare diseases [2]. This report examines how three major entities — the US, EU, and China — are positioning themselves in this rapidly evolving landscape.

### 1.2 Scope and Entity Selection

The US, EU, and China were selected because they collectively represent >80% of global mRNA R&D investment, host the majority of clinical trials, and represent distinct regulatory and market access approaches [3].

### 1.3 Methodology

This report synthesizes peer-reviewed literature (Nature, The Lancet, Science), regulatory databases (FDA, EMA, NMPA), industry reports (McKinsey, BCG), and authoritative news sources (Reuters, STAT News). Sources are graded on a five-star credibility scale (see Appendix B).
